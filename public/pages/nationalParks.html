NATIONAL PARKS AND MONUMENTS

Ever wondered if there's more to Arizona than just deserts and cacti? Here are the top 5 national parks you should check out!

1. Grand Canyon National Park

Where in Arizona?

The Grand Canyon is located in northern Arizona.

Why is this special to Arizona?

The Grand Canyon is one of the most iconic natural landmarks in the world and serves as a testament to the geological history of Arizona. Its vast expanse, intricate rock formations, and vibrant colors make it a must-visit destination for nature lovers and adventurers alike.

History:

Grand Canyon National Park was established in 1919, making it one of the oldest national parks in the United States. Its creation was driven by the need to protect the unique geological features and stunning landscapes of the Grand Canyon, ensuring that future generations could enjoy its beauty for years to come.

2. Petrified Forest National Park

Where in Arizona?

The Petrified Forest is located in northeastern Arizona.

Why is this special to Arizona?

The Petrified Forest is home to one of the largest and most colorful concentrations of petrified wood in the world, offering visitors a glimpse into Arizona's ancient past. Its unique landscape, filled with fossilized trees and vibrant rock formations, showcases the natural beauty and geological diversity of the state.

History:

Petrified Forest National Park was established in 1906 to protect the vast deposits of petrified wood found within its boundaries. The park's unique geological formations and fossilized remains provide valuable insights into the ancient ecosystems that once thrived in this region, making it an important site for scientific research and education.

3. Saguaro National Park

Where in Arizona?

Saguaro National Park is located in southern Arizona.

Why is this special to Arizona?

Saguaro National Park is home to the iconic saguaro cactus, a symbol of the American Southwest and a testament to the resilience of desert ecosystems. Its sprawling desert landscapes, dotted with towering cacti and diverse wildlife, showcase the natural beauty and cultural heritage of Arizona.

History:

Saguaro National Park was established in 1933 to protect and preserve the iconic saguaro cactus and the unique Sonoran Desert ecosystem. The park's rugged terrain and rich biodiversity make it a popular destination for outdoor enthusiasts, hikers, and nature lovers seeking to explore the beauty of the desert landscape.

4. Canyon de Chelly National Monument

Where in Arizona?

Canyon de Chelly National Monument is located in northeastern Arizona.

Why is this special to Arizona?

Canyon de Chelly National Monument is a sacred and culturally significant site that preserves the rich history and traditions of the Navajo people. Its towering sandstone cliffs, ancient ruins, and petroglyphs offer a glimpse into the region's indigenous heritage and spiritual connection to the land.

History:

Canyon de Chelly National Monument was established in 1931 to protect the unique geological and archaeological features found within its boundaries. The canyon has been inhabited by indigenous peoples for thousands of years, with evidence of human presence dating back to prehistoric times. Today, it remains an important cultural and historical site, offering visitors a window into the ancient past and the enduring traditions of the Navajo people.

5. Monument Valley Navajo Tribal Park

Where in Arizona?

Monument Valley Navajo Tribal Park straddles the Arizona-Utah border.

Why is this special to Arizona?

Monument Valley Navajo Tribal Park is a symbol of the rugged beauty and cultural heritage of the American Southwest. Its iconic rock formations, vast desert landscapes, and rich Native American history have captivated visitors for generations, offering a glimpse into the timeless beauty of the Arizona landscape.

History:

Monument Valley Navajo Tribal Park has been inhabited by indigenous peoples for thousands of years, with evidence of human presence dating back to ancient times. The park was established in 1958 to preserve the natural and cultural resources of the region, including its iconic rock formations, ancient petroglyphs, and traditional Navajo way of life. Today, it remains a sacred and cherished landscape, attracting visitors from around the world with its awe-inspiring beauty and cultural significance.
